Research activities

Starting October 1, 2020, I am pursuing a PhD at the Laboratory of Innovation in Surface Chemistry and Nanosciences (LICSEN) which is part of a French public energy research institute called the French Alternative Energies and Atomic Energy Commission (CEA). The site is in Saclay, which is a few decaminutes south of Paris. Prior to my PhD I completed a Master's degree in Materials Physics in a joint program with the first year at Uppsala University and the second year at Campus Pierre et Marie Curie of Sorbonne Universit√© (Paris 6), with the SMNO/Nanomat program. My Bachelor's degrees in Physics and Violin Performance are from Boston University.

My research activities so far have focused on studying nanomaterials, specifically 2-dimensional (2D) materials. Other types of nanomaterials include nanoparticles (0D) and nanowires (1D). Such materials have one or more dimensions confined to the nanometer-scale. The confinement effect gives rise to material properties which differ dramatically from the bulk (3D) counterpart, meaning that the nanomaterial version of a bulk material can be seen as a different material entirely. One example of this is with graphene, which is one atomic layer of graphite, about 0.34 nm thick. In the below sections I elaborate on the nanomaterial research I have been involved with.

Raman spectroscopy of graphene

During my time at Boston University, I worked with my colleagues on novel techniques using Raman spectroscopy to probe charge density of graphene. Our submission is currently in review, but the arXiv preprint is available, titled Ultra-low doping and local charge variation in graphene measured by Raman: experiment and simulation.
